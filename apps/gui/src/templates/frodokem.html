Origins & history

FrodoKEM is a conservative KEM based on plain (unstructured) Learning With Errors (LWE), designed to avoid potential pitfalls of extra algebraic structure found in Ring-/Module-LWE variants. FrodoKEM reached NIST’s Round 3 as a finalist; in the March 2025 Round-4 status report, NIST proceeded with HQC as the additional KEM, while FrodoKEM remains a well-studied alternative with active drafts and implementations. [1][2]

Design overview

FrodoKEM builds a CPA PKE over matrices \( \mathbf{A}\in \mathbb{Z}_q^{n\times n} \) and short secrets, then wraps it in a Fujisaki–Okamoto-style transform for IND-CCA2 security. The public matrix \( \mathbf{A} \) is derived via either AES-CTR or SHAKE; errors follow rounded discrete Gaussians \( \chi \). Encapsulation samples a short seed/message, encrypts it to a ciphertext pair \((\mathbf{c}_1,\mathbf{c}_2)\), and derives the shared key from hashed inputs; decapsulation uses the secret to recover the seed/message and re-derive the key with confirmation. [1]

Mathematical setting

Work in \( \mathbb{Z}_q \) with dimensions \( n\in\{640,976,1344\} \) and \( m{=}n \). Moduli are \( q{=}2^{15}{=}32768 \) for Frodo-640, and \( q{=}2^{16}{=}65536 \) for Frodo-976/1344. The error distributions \( \chi_{\text{Frodo-}n} \) are rounded discrete Gaussians with parameters tuned per level. [1]

Key relation

Sample short secrets \( \mathbf{S},\mathbf{E}\in\mathbb{Z}_q^{n\times n} \) and define

\( \mathbf{B}=\mathbf{A}\mathbf{S}+\mathbf{E}. \)

The public key is \( (\text{seed}_A,\mathbf{B}) \) (or \( \mathbf{A} \) explicitly); the secret key stores \( \mathbf{S} \) and the public key hash. [1]

Parameter sets (FrodoKEM-640/976/1344; AES or SHAKE)

All sets target IND-CCA2 security; AES/SHAKE variants differ only in how \( \mathbf{A} \) is generated.

Algorithms

Key generation

Derive \( \mathbf{A} \) from a seed via AES-CTR or SHAKE. Sample short \( \mathbf{S},\mathbf{E} \) from \( \chi \); compute \( \mathbf{B}=\mathbf{A}\mathbf{S}+\mathbf{E} \). Set \( \text{pk}=(\text{seed}_A,\mathbf{B}) \) and \( \text{sk} \) to include \( \mathbf{S} \), a hash of pk, and (per NIST API convention) pk itself. [1]

Encapsulation (CPA core + FO)

From input entropy, derive a message/seed \( \mu \) and ephemeral secrets; encrypt producing \((\mathbf{c}_1,\mathbf{c}_2)\) and compute the shared secret via KDF on authenticated inputs (binding pk and ciphertext). [1]

Decapsulation

Use \( \mathbf{S} \) to recover \( \mu \) from \((\mathbf{c}_1,\mathbf{c}_2)\); recompute and verify the encapsulation hash bindings (key confirmation). Output the agreed key or a fallback if checks fail, ensuring CCA security. [1]

Security at a glance

  • Assumptions: Plain LWE over \( \mathbb{Z}_q \) with conservative parameters; proofs in the (Q)ROM via FO-style transform. [1]
  • Categories: 640 → Cat-1, 976 → Cat-3, 1344 → Cat-5 (claimed in submission). [1]
  • Variants: AES vs SHAKE only affects \( \mathbf{A} \) generation; cryptographic core identical. [1]
  • NIST status: Not selected for standardization in Round-4; HQC chosen to augment KEM portfolio (Mar 2025). [2]

Implementation notes

  • Symmetric: SHAKE-128/256 and/or AES-128-CTR for matrix generation; KDF/PRFs per spec. [1]
  • Perf: Cost dominated by large matrix ops; AVX2 and careful memory layout help. Keys/CTs are larger than ML-KEM. [3]
  • Side-channels: Constant-time sampling/multiplication; avoid secret-dependent branches; strong input validation and key confirmation. [1]
  • Ecosystem: Available in liboqs with OpenSSL provider demos; active IETF Internet-Drafts explore usage contexts. [3][4]

Pros & cons

Pros

  • Conservative security on unstructured LWE; avoids additional algebraic assumptions. [1]
  • Simple, constant-time friendly operations (matrix adds/mults, SHAKE/AES). [1]
  • Mature reference and optimized (AVX2) implementations; straightforward to reason about. [1][3]

Cons

  • Larger public keys and ciphertexts than ML-KEM/HQC; higher bandwidth and memory costs. [3]
  • Slower due to big matrix arithmetic; careful engineering needed for constrained devices. [3]
