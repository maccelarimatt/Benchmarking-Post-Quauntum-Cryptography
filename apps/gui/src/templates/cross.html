Origins & history

CROSS is a code-based digital signature derived from a zero-knowledge identification protocol via the Fiat–Shamir transform. It was submitted to NIST’s additional signature process with two related hardness instantiations: R-SDP and R-SDP(G) (restricted syndrome decoding problems). Public documentation and updated specs were posted in 2023–2025 alongside a formal EUF-CMA proof sketch and discussion of attacks and parameterization. [1][2][3]

Design overview

CROSS commits to code-based statements and uses two challenges per round inside a parallel repetition framework. After applying Fiat–Shamir, a signature consists of (i) seeds/commitments and Merkle proofs that bind per-round transcripts, and (ii) opened responses for a chosen subset of rounds. Security relies on the difficulty of decoding restricted error vectors (values lie in a subgroup) consistent with a public parity-check matrix. The design trades runtime for signature size: more rounds \(t\) yield smaller signatures, fewer rounds yield faster signing. [1][2]

Mathematical setting

Work over finite fields \( \mathbb{F}_p \) and a cyclic subgroup \( E=\langle g\rangle \subset \mathbb{F}_p^\ast \) of order \( z \). A linear \([n,k]\) code has parity-check matrix \( H\in \mathbb{F}_p^{(n-k)\times n} \). The Restricted Syndrome Decoding problem (R-SDP) asks, given \(H\) and a syndrome \( s\in\mathbb{F}_p^{\,n-k}\), to find an error vector \( e\in E^n \) (coordinates restricted to \(E\)) of maximum Hamming weight such that

\( s \equiv e\,H^{\top} \pmod{p}. \)

Algorithms

Key generation

Sample seeds; expand a parity-check matrix \(H\) (and \(M_G\) for R-SDP(G)) from a public seed. Sample the secret restricted error \(e\in E^n\) (or \(e\in G\subset E^n\)); compute the public syndrome \( s=eH^{\top} \).

\( \mathrm{sk}=\text{Seed}_{\mathrm{sk}},\quad \mathrm{pk}=(\text{Seed}_{\mathrm{pk}},\, s). \)

Signing (Fiat–Shamir over parallel rounds)

Using a seed-tree, derive per-round randomness for \(t\) parallel executions. Compute commitments (two-challenge structure) and a Merkle tree over leaves. Hash to derive: (i) first challenge vector \( \beta\in (\mathbb{F}_p^\ast)^t \) and (ii) a binary vector \( b\in \{0,1\}^t \) of Hamming weight \(w\). Open the subset \( J=\{i\mid b_i=1\} \) and include necessary seeds, responses, and Merkle paths.

\( \sigma = (\text{Salt},\, h,\, c_0,\, c_1,\, \text{MerkleProofs},\, \text{SeedPath},\, \text{rsp}_0,\, \text{rsp}_1). \)

Verification

Expand \(H\) (and \(M_G\)) from \( \text{Seed}_{\mathrm{pk}} \). Recompute challenges \((\beta,b)\) from the transcript and message, verify Merkle proofs and per-round algebraic checks, and ensure all opened paths are consistent with \( s=eH^{\top} \) under the restrictions. Accept iff all checks pass. [1]

Security at a glance

  • Assumption: Hardness of Restricted Syndrome Decoding (R-SDP / R-SDP(G)) in the Hamming metric. [1]
  • Model: EUF-CMA in the (Q)ROM via Fiat–Shamir on a two-challenge ZK identification protocol with parallel repetition. [1][2]
  • Categories: Parameter sets for NIST Cat-1/3/5 with analyses against BJMM-style & Stern/Dumer-type decoders and dedicated forgery bounds. [1]
  • Notes: Public updates discuss proofs and evaluation of attacks (including recent analyses), with corresponding parameter guidance. [2][3][4]

Implementation notes

  • Symmetric primitives: CSPRNG = SHAKE-128/256; hashes = SHA3-256/384/512 depending on category. [1]
  • Packing: Bit-packing of \(\mathbb{F}_p\)/\(\mathbb{F}_z\) elements reduces sizes (e.g., \(\lceil(n{-}k)\cdot 7/8\rceil\) B for \(s\) in R-SDP). [1]
  • Seeded keys: Minimal pk/sk via seeds; regenerate \(H\) (and \(M_G\)) on the fly from \(\text{Seed}_{\mathrm{pk}}\). [1]
  • Constant-time: Avoid secret-dependent branching in decoders/packing; careful Merkle implementation; follow side-channel guidance. [1][3]

Pros & cons

Pros

  • Very small public keys (seeded), seed-only secret keys; simple KAT generation. [1]
  • Signature sizes competitive with SLH-DSA while often offering faster signing. [1][4]
  • Clean Fiat–Shamir construction over well-studied code-based assumptions. [1]

Cons

  • Signatures larger than lattice schemes like ML-DSA/FN-DSA; Merkle/packing complexity. [1][4]
  • Security rests on restricted decoding variants; parameter tuning must track latest cryptanalysis. [2][3]
