Origins & history

SNOVA (“Simple NOVA”) is a simplified, non-commutative-ring adaptation of UOV with alignment tricks to shrink keys. It entered NIST’s Additional Digital Signatures track in 2023 and was announced as a Round-2 candidate on Oct 25, 2024. Round-2 specs with parameter guidance and KATs were posted in 2025. [1][2][3]

Design overview

SNOVA works over a matrix ring \( \mathbb{F}_q^{m\times m} \) and interprets UOV public quadratics with matrix coefficients. A key–randomness alignment compresses the public key by publishing only aligned components (plus a small seed), while signatures commit to hashed message data with a 16-byte salt. Security targets the hardness of solving multivariate quadratic equations over non-commutative rings and resists known MinRank-style shortcuts by parameter choice.

Mathematical setting

Let \(q=16\) (GF(16)); variables split as vinegar \(v\) and oil \(o\). Public polynomials have coefficients in the matrix ring \( \mathcal{R}=\mathrm{Mat}_m(\mathbb{F}_q) \). With secret affine maps \(S,T\) and central quadratic map \(F\), the public key is \( P = S \circ F \circ T \). Alignment lets the verifier reconstruct needed matrix blocks from a compact seed while keeping non-commutative structure for security. Hashing is done with SHAKE-256; signatures include a 16-byte salt. [2]

Digest

\( \text{digest} = \mathrm{Hash}(D),\quad \text{hash}_d = \mathrm{SHAKE256}(s_{\text{public}} \parallel \text{digest} \parallel \text{salt}). \)

Algorithms

Key generation

Sample seeds; derive \(S,T,F\) and aligned public matrices from spublic. Compute compact public data and store a seed-type secret key (48 B) or an expanded private key if precomputation is desired.

\( \mathrm{pk}=(s_{\text{public}},\, \text{aligned }P'_{ij}),\quad \mathrm{sk}_{\text{seed}} = (s_{\text{public}}, s_{\text{private}})\ (48\text{ B}). \)

Signing

Compute digest of the document, pick 16-byte salt, and hash \(h_d=\mathrm{SHAKE256}(s_{\text{public}}\| \text{digest} \| \text{salt})\). Use aligned structures to produce response values; pack the signature according to \( (n,l,q) \).

\( \sigma = (\text{salt},\ \text{packed responses/aux data}) \), with \( |\sigma| = \tfrac{n\,l}{2}\log_2 q + 16 \) bytes.

Verification

Recompute aligned matrices from spublic, derive \(h_d\) from the message and salt, and run the SNOVA verification hash equations to check acceptance.

Security at a glance

  • Assumptions: Hardness of solving multivariate quadratic systems over matrix rings; analyses vs. direct, collision, memoryless collision, and recent forgery attacks. [2]
  • Categories: Parameter sets target NIST I / III / V gate-count thresholds with documented margins. [2]
  • Cryptanalysis updates: Round-2 materials discuss MinRank distribution and address attacks by Beullens/Cabarcas et al.; continue to track literature. [2][5][6]
  • Misuse resistance: 16-byte salt per signature; SHAKE-256 hashing; seed-type keys minimize storage exposure. [2]

Implementation notes

  • Symmetric core: SHAKE-256 for hashing/PRNG; KATs provided for conformance. [2]
  • Packing: Bit-packing over GF(16) dominates size; vectorized matrix ops help throughput (e.g., NEON/AVX2). [3][4]
  • Key material: Public keys are seeded; seed-type private key is 48 B (expand on demand). [2]
  • Constant-time: Avoid secret-dependent branching in evaluation and packing; verify all lengths/indices. [2]

Pros & cons

Pros

  • Very small seed-type secret keys (48 B); compact public keys relative to classic UOV. [2]
  • Hash-and-pack friendly; good parallelism on modern CPUs (matrix ops). [3][4]
  • Active parameter evolution with transparent security analyses. [2][5][6]

Cons

  • Signatures larger than ML-DSA; packing complexity and careful implementation required. [2]
  • Ongoing cryptanalysis (e.g., MinRank/fault models) requires vigilance and potential countermeasures. [5][7]
