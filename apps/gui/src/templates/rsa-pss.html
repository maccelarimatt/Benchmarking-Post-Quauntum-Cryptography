About

RSASSA-PSS is the modern, provably secure RSA signature encoding defined in PKCS #1 v2.2 (RFC 8017) and profiled by NIST (FIPS 186-5) [1], [3]. It replaces deterministic RSASSA-PKCS1 v1.5 and achieves EUF-CMA security in the Random-Oracle Model under RSA-inversion assumptions [2].

Origins & history

RSA (1977) provides a trapdoor permutation \(x\mapsto x^e \bmod n\). In 1996, Bellare and Rogaway introduced the PSS paradigm with tight ROM proofs for RSA signatures [2]. PSS was adopted into PKCS #1 v2 (now v2.2 / RFC 8017) [1], and NIST standardized its use in FIPS 186-5 (2023) [3]. Today, RSA-PSS is the recommended RSA signature in X.509/PKIX via RFC 4055 [8].

Design overview

PSS signs a hash of the message using a randomized encoding (EMSA-PSS) that blends a per-signature salt via MGF1; the encoded message is exponentiated with \(d\), and verification recomputes the encoding and checks against the recovered representative [1], [2].

Mathematical setting

RSA keys

Generate primes \(p,q\), set \(n=pq\), choose \(e\) coprime to \(\lambda(n)=\mathrm{lcm}(p-1,q-1)\), and compute \(d\equiv e^{-1}\bmod \lambda(n)\) [1]. Signing and verification use:

\( s \equiv \mathrm{EM}^d \bmod n,\qquad \hat{\mathrm{EM}} \equiv s^e \bmod n. \)

EMSA-PSS encoding

Let \(H\) be a hash (output \(hLen\)), and MGF1 be the mask generator (based on \(H\)). With \(\text{emBits}=\lfloor \log_2(n-1)\rfloor\), \(\text{emLen}=\lceil \text{emBits}/8\rceil\): compute \(mHash=H(M)\), pick a random salt, form \(M'\), compute \(H'\), construct \(DB\), mask with MGF1, clear leading bits, and output \(\mathrm{EM}=\text{maskedDB}\,\|\,H'\,\|\,0xBC\) [1], [2].

Verification inverts these steps on \(\hat{\mathrm{EM}}\) and checks the trailer and bit-clearing conditions [1].

Parameter sets & sizes

Recommended choices

  • Hash: SHA-256 (or SHA-384/512 for higher strength) [1], [3].
  • MGF: MGF1 with the same hash as above [1].
  • Salt length: \(sLen=hLen\) (typical PKI/TLS profiles) [1], [8].
  • Public exponent: \(e=65537\) [1].

Algorithms

Key generation

Generate \(p,q\), compute \(n,\lambda(n),e,d\); store CRT values \(d_p,d_q,q^{-1}\bmod p\) for fast signing [1].

Signing (RSASSA-PSS)

  1. Compute \(mHash=H(M)\); sample random \(\text{salt}\) [1], [2].
  2. EM \(\leftarrow\) EMSA-PSS-ENCODE\((mHash,\text{emBits},sLen)\) [1].
  3. Output \( s \equiv \mathrm{EM}^d \bmod n \) (use CRT and blinding) [1], [6], [5].

Verification

  1. Recover \(\hat{\mathrm{EM}} \equiv s^e \bmod n\) and run EMSA-PSS-VERIFY (check trailer 0xBC, leading-bit clear, and recomputed \(H'\)) [1].

Security at a glance

  • Goal: EUF-CMA in the ROM [2].
  • Assumptions: RSA-inversion hardness; \(H\) and MGF1 modeled as random oracles [2].
  • Standards: Encoding and verification rules fixed by PKCS #1 v2.2 [1]; profiled in FIPS 186-5 [3]; identifiers and use in X.509 by RFC 4055 [8].
  • Side-channels: timing-attack resistance (blinding) and fault-attack checks (e.g., re-encrypt after CRT) are required [6], [5].
  • Quantum note: RSA (incl. PSS) is broken by Shor’s algorithm; plan hybrid/PQ transition guided by NIST key-management advice [4].

Implementation notes

  • Use vetted libraries; fix \(H\), MGF1, and \(sLen\) per key (do not mix) [1], [3].
  • Strong RNG for salts; never reuse salt deterministically [1], [3].
  • Enable CRT and verify the signature with \(e\) to catch faults; use RSA blinding to mitigate timing/leakage [1], [6], [5].
  • Follow PKIX conventions/identifiers when integrating with X.509 and CMS/PKI [8].

Pros & cons

Pros

  • Provably secure in ROM with tight bounds; widely standardized (PKCS #1, FIPS, PKIX) [1]–[3], [8].
  • Interoperable in TLS, X.509, JOSE, SSH ecosystems [8].
  • Configurable via hash and salt length [1], [3].

Cons

  • Larger keys and slower private ops than many PQ/lattice signatures [4].
  • Requires high-quality randomness for salts; RNG failures are catastrophic [1], [3].
  • No post-quantum security; migration planning required [4].
